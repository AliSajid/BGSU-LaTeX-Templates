This study examines how Pakistani American immigrant families navigate through the process of identity development and negotiation through retrospective storytelling across first-, one-and-a-half and second-generations. The study marries the Communication theory of identity (CTI) and the retrospective storytelling heuristic of Communicated narrative sense-making (CNSM) theory. Using a qualitative, interpretivist, arts-based research (ABR) approach, this study employs poetic inquiry as a tool of thematic analysis. The data is collected through the method of episodic narrative in-depth interviews with 24 Pakistani Americans across the three generations. Findings reveal that retrospective storytelling creates, reveals and mediates identity gaps, enabling individuals to navigate the tensions between who they are, who they are expected to be, and how they are perceived across contexts. The study introduces communal retrospective storytelling and its function as shared decision-making infrastructure through which immigrant families collectively interpret past experiences of Pakistani community members in their surroundings to manage present uncertainty and anticipate future outcomes of their children.
